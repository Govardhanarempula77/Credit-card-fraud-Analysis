01 · Background

Business Context & Motivation

A newly launched credit card company serving the western United States has made safety its core brand promise. To back that promise with data-driven action, a machine learning model was built to automatically flag suspicious transactions before they cause harm to customers.

Executive directive: The model must err on the side of caution. Flagging a legitimate transaction as fraud is an acceptable inconvenience — but missing real fraud undermines customer trust and the company's core value proposition.

This report walks through every step of the analysis in plain language, from the raw data through to final model performance — written for a broad audience that does not require a technical background.

02 · The Data

Dataset Overview

The dataset provided by the executive contains 339,607 credit card transactions across 14 merchant categories, with cardholders located throughout the western United States. Each transaction is labelled as either legitimate or fraudulent.

Class imbalance: Only 0.52% of transactions are fraudulent (1,782 of 339,607). This extreme imbalance means a naive model that flags nothing as fraud would be 99.48% "accurate" — yet completely useless. Special techniques are required to handle this.

03 · Exploratory Analysis

What Does Fraud Look Like?

Before building any model, the data was visually explored to uncover patterns that distinguish fraudulent transactions from legitimate ones. Several strong signals emerged:

  • Transaction amount: Fraudulent transactions average $518 versus just $68 for legitimate ones — fraudsters consistently target higher-value purchases.
  • Merchant category: Grocery POS, misc net, and shopping net carry the highest fraud rates. Online/net categories are disproportionately targeted relative to their transaction volume.
  • Time of day: Fraud peaks sharply in overnight hours (midnight–3 AM) when cardholders are unlikely to notice unusual activity.
  • Distance from home: Fraudulent transactions occur significantly further from the cardholder's registered address.
  • Cardholder age: Older cardholders appear slightly more targeted, though the pattern is subtle.

04 · Methodology

Analytical Pipeline

1

Feature Engineering

New predictive features were derived: hour of day, day of week, cardholder age (from date of birth), and the straight-line distance in miles between the cardholder's home and the merchant location (computed via haversine formula from GPS coordinates).

2

Preprocessing

Numeric features were standardised (zero mean, unit variance) so no single feature dominates the model. The merchant category variable was one-hot encoded into 14 binary columns.

3

Handling Class Imbalance with SMOTE

With only 0.52% fraud, the training set was rebalanced using SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) — generating synthetic fraud examples so the model learned what fraud looks like rather than defaulting to always predicting "legitimate."

4

Model Training

Two models were trained: a Logistic Regression baseline and a Random Forest as the primary model, both with balanced class weighting. The 80/20 train-test split was stratified to maintain the fraud ratio in both sets.

5

Threshold Tuning for Caution-First Detection

The decision threshold was lowered from 0.50 to 0.30. This means the model flags a transaction as suspicious whenever it estimates a 30%+ probability of fraud — casting a wider net to ensure real fraud is not missed.

    R = 3958.8

05 · Results

Model Performance

The Random Forest model with a decision threshold of 0.30 was evaluated on a held-out test set of 67,922 transactions (containing 356 genuine fraud cases).

On recall vs. precision: The Random Forest catches 92.7% of all real fraud — meaning only 26 out of 356 genuine fraud transactions in the test set were missed. The trade-off is that 783 legitimate transactions were also flagged as suspicious. Given the executive's directive, this is the right balance: a brief customer service call to verify a legitimate transaction is far less costly than undetected fraud.

ROC-AUC of 0.993 means the model has near-perfect ability to rank fraudulent transactions above legitimate ones — placing it in the top tier of fraud detection performance for real-world datasets of this type.

Reading the Confusion Matrix

The confusion matrix for the Random Forest shows four outcomes. In the test set: 66,783 legitimate transactions were correctly cleared; 783 were incorrectly flagged (false alarms). Of the 356 real fraud cases, 330 were correctly caught and only 26 slipped through undetected. With the executive's caution-first mandate in mind, 330 caught vs. 26 missed is an excellent result.

Most Important Features

The Random Forest identified transaction amount and the distance between cardholder home and merchant as the two strongest fraud predictors — consistent with the EDA findings. Hour of day, cardholder age, and specific merchant categories (particularly net/online shopping) also contributed significantly.

06 · Conclusions

Findings & Recommendations

🎯

Caution-first objective met

92.7% of real fraud is caught. The lowered decision threshold directly delivers on the executive's directive to flag rather than miss.

📊

Near-perfect discrimination

ROC-AUC of 0.993 places this model in the top tier of fraud detection. It consistently ranks genuine fraud above legitimate transactions.

🛒

Online/net channels are highest risk

Shopping net, misc net, and online grocery carry the highest fraud rates. Extra scrutiny on these channels would yield the greatest safety gains.

🌙

Overnight transactions warrant monitoring

Fraud peaks sharply between midnight and 3 AM. Real-time alerts during these hours could intercept fraud before it completes.

📍

Distance is a powerful signal

Transactions occurring far from the cardholder's home address are a top predictor. Flagging these for secondary verification is low-cost and high-impact.

🔄

Ongoing monitoring required

Fraud patterns evolve. Monthly model retraining on fresh transaction data is recommended, with performance dashboards tracking recall and false alarm rates.

Limitations & Next Steps

This model was trained on a static historical dataset. In production, velocity features — such as the number of transactions by a single cardholder in the past hour — would likely boost recall further. Integrating a secondary human-review queue for the highest-risk flagged transactions (e.g., those scoring above 0.85 fraud probability) would improve the customer experience for false-alarm cases. A gradient boosting model (XGBoost or LightGBM) is also recommended as a next iteration, as these often outperform Random Forests on highly imbalanced tabular data.
